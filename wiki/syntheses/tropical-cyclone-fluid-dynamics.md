The new ingest adds three more constraints. First, predictability is scale and regime dependent: convective details decorrelate quickly, while the mean vortex, RI timing, and RI mode evolution remain sensitive to both large-scale environment and internal stochastic reorganization. Second, the secondary-eyewall gap is now partially closed by one rainband-PV case study, showing that mature-storm structural change can emerge from outer-core convection rather than only inner-core wave dynamics. Third, coastal wave-current-surge coupling is now better defined as its own branch: wave state, Stokes drift, radiation-stress gradients, and atmospheric-forcing choice can materially alter surface drag, transport, and storm-surge prediction, so wave coupling cannot be treated as a minor boundary condition. Potential-intensity theory remains useful as an upper-bound framework, but it is incomplete, and both strict axisymmetric theory and oversimplified eyewall-only outflow schematics miss important 3-D structural effects and wave-coupled surface-layer behavior.

# Lines Of Evidence

- The review places rotating convection near the center of modern intensification theory, with cloud buoyancy and vortex-tube stretching treated as key mechanisms.
- The review argues that the boundary layer strongly controls eyewall placement and spin-up, including vertical delivery of high tangential momentum into the eyewall updraft.
- Smith and Montgomery (2010) provide direct primary-study support for a stronger boundary-layer role, while also showing that this role depends on whether the boundary layer is modeled as steady or time dependent.
- Zhang et al. (2011) add direct dropsonde constraints on hurricane boundary-layer vertical structure, showing that inflow and agradient depth scales are distinct from shallow mixed-layer depth and therefore should not be collapsed to a single thermodynamic layer definition.
- Persing et al. (2013) provide direct idealized 3-D support for the rotating-convection branch of the current synthesis, showing that vortical plumes and counter-gradient eddy momentum fluxes can aid contraction and spin-up of the azimuthal-mean vortex.
- Nolan and Montgomery (2002) and Nolan and Grasso (2003) add a companion nonhydrostatic perturbation framework showing how asymmetric perturbation evolution and symmetric adjustment can be analyzed consistently in balanced hurricane-like vortices.

- Stern and Nolan (2009) add an inner-core structural constraint that eyewall slope and vertical decay of maximum tangential wind depend strongly on storm size and thermodynamic structure, not just intensity class.
- Stern and Nolan (2012) report midlevel warm-core maxima in idealized simulations and limited intensity dependence of warm-core height across tested strengths.
- Stern et al. (2015) report that eyewall contraction and intensification can decouple after early spin-up, so contraction is an incomplete standalone proxy for continuing intensity increase.
- Nolan et al. (2007) add an energetics constraint for asymmetric forcing in idealized vortices: the azimuthal-mean heating response dominates mean intensification, while purely asymmetric heating often produces weak net effects or weakening.
- Consistent with this branch, Nolan and Grasso (2003) report that asymmetric temperature perturbations produce much smaller net intensity effects than equal-amplitude symmetric perturbations in their companion experiments.
- Emanuel (1986) and Emanuel (1988) provide the foundational and classic potential-intensity formulations that connect environmental thermodynamics to a theoretical maximum intensity.
- Bryan and Rotunno (2009a, 2009b) test PI-style analytical assumptions against axisymmetric numerical simulations and show that inferred maximum intensity is highly assumption sensitive.
- Emanuel and Rotunno (2011) revise PI framing by treating outflow stratification as partly internally constrained by storm structure.
- Nolan et al. (2025) add a new structural constraint on mature storms: in two simulations, surrounding rainbands supply much of the outflow dry-air mass and most of the condensate in the cirrus shield, so upper-level outflow is not simply eyewall exhaust.
- The same paper argues that repeated rainband and mesoscale injections complicate PI-style treatments that assume a relatively self-contained outflow layer with internally set stratification.
- Powell et al. (2003) report reduced effective high-wind drag in tropical cyclones relative to simple extrapolations from weaker winds.
- Donelan et al. (2004) report a limiting aerodynamic roughness regime in very strong winds, supporting drag saturation behavior.
- Holthuijsen et al. (2012) connect high-wind exchange behavior to explicit wind-wave structure in extreme hurricanes rather than wind speed alone.
- Curcic and Haus (2020) revise strong-wind drag estimates and report saturation near Cd ~2.6 x 10^-3 around U10 ~25 m s^-1 in corrected processing.
- Price (1981) established the canonical upper-ocean cold-wake mechanism beneath moving hurricanes, including storm-relative asymmetry tied to inertial response and entrainment.
- Schade and Emanuel (1999) showed in a simple coupled framework that storm-induced cooling can substantially reduce simulated intensity relative to uncoupled runs.
- Bender and Ginis (2000) extended this in real-case forecasts, showing that coupling and cold-wake representation can reduce over-intensification errors.
- Chan et al. (2001) showed that a reduced-order coupled model can still reproduce first-order cold-wake intensity damping behavior.
- Cione and Uhlhorn (2003) add observational support that hurricane-environment SST heterogeneity is large enough to matter for intensity tendency diagnosis.
- Wright et al. (2001) and Walsh et al. (2002) provide directional-spectrum benchmarks showing strong storm-relative wave-field heterogeneity in open-ocean and landfall settings.
- Walsh et al. (2021) show that NOAA WSRA can map these directional-spectrum asymmetries at high density in real time during reconnaissance, including right-front-quadrant wave maxima in a Hurricane Lorenzo case.
- Chen and Curcic (2016) show that wave spectra become broader and more complex near landfall than in the open ocean and that wind-wave misalignment in shallow water can materially modify modeled drag.
- Curcic et al. (2016) show from Hurricane Isaac drifters and a coupled model that Stokes drift contributes about 20% of the near-surface Lagrangian flow and sharply increases lateral dispersion relative to prestorm conditions.
- Dietrich et al. (2018) show that storm-surge forecasts in Isaac depended strongly on atmospheric-forcing construction and improved when forced by a full-physics coupled atmosphere-wave-ocean model.
- Li et al. (2019) show in a coupled Earl system that RI sensitivity is tied most strongly to initial mean wind strength and asymmetry orientation, while stochastic environmental forcing widens track spread more than internal-vortex perturbations alone.
- Dobbelaere et al. (2022) show that coastal wave-current coupling during Irma can change modeled currents by up to about 1 m s^-1 and alter drifting-material pathways by kilometers to tens of kilometers.
- Judt and Chen (2010) add a first secondary-eyewall branch by arguing that convectively generated rainband PV in Hurricane Rita created a secondary outer-vortex maximum and that VRWs were not the initiating mechanism in that case.
- Judt et al. (2016) show that forecast-error growth is strongly scale dependent, with convective asymmetries saturating much faster than the mean vortex and wave number-1 asymmetry.
- Judt and Chen (2016) show that RI timing has a stochastic component even when the likelihood of RI and eventual major-hurricane intensity is high across an ensemble.
- Judt et al. (2023) argue that RI occurs in at least two structural modes, one symmetric and one burst-driven, which helps reconcile disagreement about whether convective bursts and strong-shear RI are exceptional or fundamental.
- Fischer et al. (2025) provide the first multicase observational RI anchor in the wiki, showing that rapid intensification occurs preferentially in tall, narrow, vertically aligned vortices with deeper upper-tropospheric ascent and greater convective-burst coverage after controlling for current intensity.
- The same paper shows that favorable vortex structure and favorable environment each contribute unique information about future intensification rate, so RI is not well described by environmental indices or inner-core structure alone.
- Hazelton et al. (2018, 2021) add a system-development branch showing that FV3/HAFS-class global-nest frameworks can deliver competitive track skill and improving intensity guidance, while RI and transition-regime forecast errors remain concentrated failure modes.
- Hazelton et al. (2020) add a process-level sheared-RI signal from high-resolution ensembles: reduced tilt and upshear humidification distinguish stronger members even under similar bulk shear.

- The paper treats mature intensity and maximum intensity as partially constrained by PI theory, while emphasizing sensitivity to boundary-layer assumptions, turbulence, and 3D effects.
- The review argues that sustained global steady state is physically problematic unless the source of cyclonic relative angular momentum is identified explicitly.

# Disagreement And Caveats

- This synthesis still leans heavily on one review article, so much of its structure remains a map of the literature as the reviewers present it rather than a full wiki-level adjudication across primary studies.
- The ingested primary papers now include one theoretical boundary-layer study, one idealized 3-D versus axisymmetric modeling study, one mature-outflow structural study, a four-paper Falko-Judt-led predictability/RI-mode/secondary-eyewall chain (2010-2023), a five-paper Michael-Fischer-led RI chain (2018-2025), and a three-paper Andrew-Hazelton-led FV3/HAFS numerical-modeling chain (2018-2021) spanning seasonal verification and sheared-RI ensemble process diagnostics.
- The review article intentionally omits several major topics: genesis, storm motion, environmental shear interaction, secondary eyewalls, extratropical transition, and microphysics. The wiki now partially closes the ocean-feedback and wave-coupling gap through four high-wind drag papers, three hurricane directional-wave-spectrum papers, and a five-paper Curcic-linked wave-coupled transport/surge/predictability chain. It also partially closes the secondary-eyewall/predictability gap through one Rita case study plus three predictability/RI-mode papers. Coverage is still first-pass and remains thin on concentric-eyewall climatology, shear-interaction observations, and microphysics.
- The new wave-coupling branch is still weighted toward model-based case studies plus one unusually rich drifter event, so direct observational closure of coastal wave-current-surge feedbacks remains thinner than the theory and simulation coverage.
- Some of the strongest claims in the current wiki still rely on model-based or theory-based arguments, and even the new RI paper does not yet resolve RI-onset chronology or the lowest-boundary-layer flow directly.

# Implications

- The next high-value ingests are direct observational spin-up and RI-onset studies, concentric-eyewall and eyewall-replacement papers, TC-RADAR-based vortex-tilt and convective-burst papers, predictability studies outside Hurricane Earl, potential-intensity theory tests, and steady-state or radiative-convective-equilibrium simulations.
- The wiki should expand next by adding primary-study pages before broadening into storms, basins, or datasets.
