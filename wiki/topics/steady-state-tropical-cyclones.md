# Definition

A steady-state tropical cyclone is a vortex whose macroscale structure does not vary systematically with time. The review distinguishes this strong global notion from more limited inner-core quasi-steady behavior.

# Current Synthesis

[Montgomery and Smith (2017)](../papers/montgomery-smith-2017-fluid-dynamics-of-tropical-cyclones.md) argue that a realistic globally steady tropical cyclone is doubtful. Their synthesis is that friction continually removes angular momentum at the surface, so a steady storm would require a sustained source of cyclonic relative angular momentum and additional dynamical constraints outside the boundary layer.

The paper does not say sustained simulations are impossible. Instead, it argues that any such quasi-steady solutions require an identified source of cyclonic relative angular momentum. In some axisymmetric experiments that source appears linked to vertical diffusion in the anticyclonic outer vortex; in other long-lived simulations the source remains unclear.

# Evidence By Source

- Montgomery and Smith (2017) state that without a steady source of cyclonic relative angular momentum, tropical cyclones must be globally transient.
- The review notes that the limitation of the steady-state assumption was recognized earlier for all but the inner area of a mature cyclone.
- The paper discusses axisymmetric and 3D long-duration simulated hurricanes, but emphasizes that the needed source of cyclonic relative angular momentum was not always identified.

# Open Questions

- Can a physically realistic 3D or coupled model produce a sustained hurricane while closing the angular-momentum budget cleanly?
- What is the right distinction between transient, quasi-steady, and statistically steady behavior in hurricane simulations?
- How much of the classic heat-engine framing depends on assuming a quasi-steady state that may not exist globally?
