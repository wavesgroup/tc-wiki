# Definition

The rotating-convection paradigm treats tropical cyclone intensification as an intrinsically 3D process in which localized rotating deep convection amplifies and reorganizes vorticity within the developing vortex.

# Current Synthesis

[Montgomery and Smith (2017)](../papers/montgomery-smith-2017-fluid-dynamics-of-tropical-cyclones.md) identify this paradigm as the main emphasis of their review. In their summary, cloud buoyancy and vortex-tube stretching are key ingredients. Convective updrafts locally amplify vertical vorticity, and the resulting cyclonic anomalies can consolidate into a stronger inner-core vortex.

The review also stresses that the stochastic, asymmetric nature of deep convection matters. This is one reason the authors argue that strictly axisymmetric convective-ring frameworks have intrinsic limitations for understanding intensification.

[Persing et al. (2013)](../papers/persing-et-al-2013-asymmetric-and-axisymmetric-dynamics-of-tropical-cyclones.md) provide direct primary-study support for that argument in the prototype problem. Their 3-D simulations show convection being strained tangentially by the vortex's differential angular rotation rather than immediately organizing into concentric rings. The resulting vortical plumes contribute to azimuthally averaged heating and to eddy momentum transports that can help contract the radius of maximum wind.

[Nolan and Montgomery (2002)](../papers/nolan-montgomery-2002-nonhydrostatic-three-dimensional-perturbations-part-i.md) and [Nolan and Grasso (2003)](../papers/nolan-grasso-2003-nonhydrostatic-three-dimensional-perturbations-part-ii.md) provide a companion nonhydrostatic perturbation framework that helps formalize how asymmetric eddies interact with symmetric vortex structure in idealized hurricane-like flows.

[Nolan et al. (2007)](../papers/nolan-moon-stern-2007-tropical-cyclone-intensification-from-asymmetric-convection.md) complement that result with an energy-budget perspective: localized asymmetric heating does not automatically spin up the mean vortex efficiently, because evolving asymmetries can extract kinetic energy from the symmetric circulation during transient growth.

[Fischer et al. (2025)](../papers/fischer-et-al-2025-rapidly-intensifying-tropical-cyclones-vortex-convective-characteristics.md) provide multicase observational support for the convective side of this picture in real storms. Their rapidly intensifying composites show stronger mid-upper-tropospheric ascent and much larger convective-burst coverage in the eyewall than non-RI composites, although the paper does not yet determine whether those bursts initiate RI or intensify within an already favorable vortex.

# Evidence By Source

- Montgomery and Smith (2017) define the paradigm around localized rotating deep convection growing in a cyclonic, vorticity-rich environment.
- The review states that convective updrafts greatly amplify local vertical vorticity through vortex-tube stretching and that patches of enhanced cyclonic vorticity can aggregate into a central vorticity monolith.
- The authors further state that observational evidence is accumulating that convective bursts in predepression disturbances and tropical cyclones spin up localized lower-tropospheric cyclonic vorticity anomalies.
- The review argues that strictly axisymmetric models can overstate convective efficiency and spin-up rate relative to 3D simulations.
- [Persing et al. (2013)](../papers/persing-et-al-2013-asymmetric-and-axisymmetric-dynamics-of-tropical-cyclones.md) find that 3-D vortical plume structures assist intensification by contributing to radial contraction of the maximum tangential wind and upward extension of strong tangential winds.
- The same paper shows that local heating in the 3-D model can exceed that in the axisymmetric model even while the azimuthal-mean heating rate remains weaker because convection is less ring-like.
- [Nolan and Montgomery (2002)](../papers/nolan-montgomery-2002-nonhydrostatic-three-dimensional-perturbations-part-i.md) establish the linear nonhydrostatic asymmetric-perturbation formulation used to diagnose how balanced hurricane-like vortices respond to 3-D disturbances.
- [Nolan and Grasso (2003)](../papers/nolan-grasso-2003-nonhydrostatic-three-dimensional-perturbations-part-ii.md) show that asymmetric perturbations can force symmetric tendencies through wave-mean-flow interactions, with net intensity effects smaller than direct symmetric forcing in their tested setups.
- [Nolan et al. (2007)](../papers/nolan-moon-stern-2007-tropical-cyclone-intensification-from-asymmetric-convection.md) show that purely asymmetric heating generally yields weak or negative net mean-intensity tendencies in their baseline idealized experiments.
- The same paper nevertheless identifies parameter regimes where asymmetric forcing can contribute positively, while reaffirming that azimuthal-mean heating remains the dominant pathway for mean-vortex intensification in their framework.
- [Fischer et al. (2025)](../papers/fischer-et-al-2025-rapidly-intensifying-tropical-cyclones-vortex-convective-characteristics.md) show that rapidly intensifying storms have a much larger number and fractional eyewall coverage of convective bursts than slowly intensifying or nonintensifying storms.
- The same paper also finds that RI storms have deeper anomalous ascent in the mid-upper troposphere, which is consistent with rotating-convection arguments that emphasize sustained deep inner-core convection rather than shallow ascent alone.

# Open Questions

- How do local convective vorticity anomalies upscale quantitatively into storm-scale intensification?
- Which diagnostics best separate genuine eddy-driven spin-up from artifacts of azimuthal averaging?
- How should this framework be connected to operational rapid-intensification guidance?
- Do convective bursts lead RI onset, or are they amplified by a vortex that is already tall, narrow, and aligned?
