# Definition

The rotating-convection paradigm treats tropical cyclone intensification as an intrinsically 3D process in which localized rotating deep convection amplifies and reorganizes vorticity within the developing vortex.

# Current Synthesis

[Montgomery and Smith (2017)](../papers/montgomery-smith-2017-fluid-dynamics-of-tropical-cyclones.md) identify this paradigm as the main emphasis of their review. In their summary, cloud buoyancy and vortex-tube stretching are key ingredients. Convective updrafts locally amplify vertical vorticity, and the resulting cyclonic anomalies can consolidate into a stronger inner-core vortex.

The review also stresses that the stochastic, asymmetric nature of deep convection matters. This is one reason the authors argue that strictly axisymmetric convective-ring frameworks have intrinsic limitations for understanding intensification.

# Evidence By Source

- Montgomery and Smith (2017) define the paradigm around localized rotating deep convection growing in a cyclonic, vorticity-rich environment.
- The review states that convective updrafts greatly amplify local vertical vorticity through vortex-tube stretching and that patches of enhanced cyclonic vorticity can aggregate into a central vorticity monolith.
- The authors further state that observational evidence is accumulating that convective bursts in predepression disturbances and tropical cyclones spin up localized lower-tropospheric cyclonic vorticity anomalies.
- The review argues that strictly axisymmetric models can overstate convective efficiency and spin-up rate relative to 3D simulations.

# Open Questions

- How do local convective vorticity anomalies upscale quantitatively into storm-scale intensification?
- Which diagnostics best separate genuine eddy-driven spin-up from artifacts of azimuthal averaging?
- How should this framework be connected to operational rapid-intensification guidance?
