# Definition

Atlantic hurricane climate controls and seasonal forecasting here refers to the interannual and intraseasonal large-scale modes that modulate Atlantic tropical cyclone activity, rapid intensification, and U.S. landfall risk, as well as the forecast frameworks that turn those environmental signals into basin-scale seasonal outlooks.

# Current Synthesis

The Klotzbach-centered branch added here argues that Atlantic tropical cyclone predictability extends well beyond intrinsic storm-scale chaos. [Klotzbach (2010)](../papers/klotzbach-2010-mjo-atlantic-hurricane-relationship.md) shows that the Madden-Julian oscillation (MJO) materially modulates Atlantic genesis, intensification, Gulf/Caribbean activity, and U.S. landfall probability on roughly 2-week time scales through coherent changes in shear and moisture. [Klotzbach (2011)](../papers/klotzbach-2011-enso-atlantic-hurricanes-us-landfalls.md) then extends the background-state argument to ENSO on seasonal time scales, showing large shifts in Atlantic hurricane activity and in East Coast versus Gulf landfall probabilities between El Niño and La Niña years.

[Klotzbach (2012)](../papers/klotzbach-2012-enso-mjo-atlantic-ri.md) makes the key bridge to the wiki's existing rapid-intensification branch by showing that these same climate modes also modulate Atlantic RI probability. In this framing, RI remains a storm-structural problem, but the basinwide odds of RI are still conditioned by subseasonal and seasonal background state.

[Jones et al. (2020)](../papers/jones-bell-klotzbach-2020-na-atlantic-vws-seasonal-tc-activity.md) provide a mechanism-oriented extension by decomposing boreal-summer tropical North Atlantic vertical wind shear into modes linked not only to ENSO and the Atlantic meridional mode but also to subtropical anticyclonic Rossby wave breaking. This matters because it broadens Atlantic seasonal TC predictability from a purely tropical teleconnection story into a tropical-extratropical environmental-regime story.

[Klotzbach et al. (2020)](../papers/klotzbach-caron-bell-2020-statistical-dynamical-north-atlantic-seasonal-hurricane-prediction.md) operationalize that climate-controls branch. Rather than predicting storms directly, the paper uses a dynamical seasonal model to forecast the large-scale predictors in CSU's August statistical scheme, then regresses those predictors onto seasonal ACE. Inference for this wiki: Atlantic seasonal forecast skill is partly a predictor-forecast problem, not only a storm forecast problem.

Taken together, these papers support a layered Atlantic predictability hierarchy. MJO phase shifts subseasonal odds of genesis and landfall-favorable genesis geography; ENSO shifts the seasonal background through shear, moisture, and circulation changes; and hybrid forecast systems gain skill when they predict those environmental controls directly. This does not solve storm-scale RI timing or structure prediction, but it does show that Atlantic TC activity has meaningful climate-mode conditioning from roughly 2-week to seasonal lead times.

# Evidence By Source

- [Klotzbach (2010)](../papers/klotzbach-2010-mjo-atlantic-hurricane-relationship.md) shows that MJO phases 1-2 are much more favorable than phases 6-7 for Atlantic genesis, major-hurricane activity, Gulf/Caribbean storm formation, and U.S. landfalls.
- The same paper ties those activity changes to coherent MJO-phase modulation of tropical Atlantic shear and humidity.
- [Klotzbach (2011)](../papers/klotzbach-2011-enso-atlantic-hurricanes-us-landfalls.md) shows that La Niña years have substantially more Atlantic hurricanes, major hurricanes, and ACE than El Niño years.
- The same paper finds that ENSO modulation is stronger in the Caribbean than over the rest of the tropical Atlantic and that East Coast landfall probabilities respond more sharply than Gulf Coast probabilities.
- [Klotzbach (2012)](../papers/klotzbach-2012-enso-mjo-atlantic-ri.md) shows that Atlantic RI events are more frequent in La Niña years and in favorable MJO phases, especially when those modes align.
- [Jones et al. (2020)](../papers/jones-bell-klotzbach-2020-na-atlantic-vws-seasonal-tc-activity.md) show that ENSO dominates the leading tropical North Atlantic shear mode, while anticyclonic Rossby wave breaking drives the second mode and contributes materially to seasonal TC activity.
- [Klotzbach et al. (2020)](../papers/klotzbach-caron-bell-2020-statistical-dynamical-north-atlantic-seasonal-hurricane-prediction.md) show that seasonal dynamical forecasts of CSU predictor fields can be turned into skillful seasonal ACE outlooks and that combining statistical and statistical/dynamical models improves skill further.

# Open Questions

- Which large-scale Atlantic climate controls are most forecastable at 2-6 week lead times, and which remain too regime dependent for stable operational use?
- How much of the climate-mode signal on RI is mediated through genesis location and track geography versus direct modulation of inner-core ventilation and moisture?
- Can seasonal forecast systems move beyond ACE to skillful prediction of major-hurricane counts, RI frequency, or regional landfall risk without overfitting?
- How stable are Atlantic climate-control relationships under recent multidecadal background-state changes and observing-system changes?
