# Research Question

Stern and Nolan (2009) reassess how tangential winds decay with height in tropical cyclones, how eyewall/RMW slope relates to storm structure, and whether widely used conceptual assumptions about vertical wind structure are dynamically self-consistent.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Theory-plus-observation-constrained structure analysis.
- Conceptual framework: Thermal-wind balance and slantwise moist-neutral constraints for inner-core structure.
- Observational context: Aircraft-era tropical-cyclone structural datasets discussed in the article framing (full observational details should be added after direct AMS full-text extraction).
- Diagnostics emphasized in accessible records: Vertical structure of tangential wind maxima, RMW slope behavior, and links to angular-momentum (M) surface geometry.

# Findings

- Stern and Nolan (2009) argue that RMW slope is primarily a function of storm size and not intensity when thermal-wind and slantwise-neutral constraints are both imposed.
- The paper reexamines the common assumption that tangential-wind maxima simply decay upward in a fixed way and instead frames decay behavior as coupled to inner-core thermodynamic structure.
- In the cited description, the authors report that Emanuel-theory-consistent structure implies the RMW should closely follow an M surface through much of the vortex depth.
