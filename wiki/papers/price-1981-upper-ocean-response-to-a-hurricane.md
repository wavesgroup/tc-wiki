Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary physical oceanography study.

# Research Question

Price (1981) asks how a moving hurricane forces mixed-layer currents, entrainment, and sea-surface cooling, and how those responses vary with storm translation speed and track-relative position.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Upper-ocean process analysis using historical hurricane forcing and a three-dimensional mixed-layer response framework.
- Core focus: Wind-driven inertial response, entrainment at the mixed-layer base, and SST evolution beneath a translating storm.
- Diagnostic outputs: Mixed-layer velocity structure, cooling magnitude, and right-of-track asymmetry in upper-ocean response.

# Findings

- Price (1981) established a canonical picture in which strongest upper-ocean cooling and mixing occur preferentially on the right side of storm motion in the Northern Hemisphere because near-inertial current response and wind forcing align more efficiently there.
- The study showed that storm-forced entrainment and vertical mixing can cool the ocean surface quickly enough to alter subsequent air-sea enthalpy fluxes available to the cyclone.
- Inference for this wiki: this paper is a foundational mechanism link in the cold-wake negative feedback pathway later used in coupled intensity studies.

# Limitations

- This is a process-oriented ocean study, not an atmosphere-ocean coupled intensity forecast experiment.
- The ingest is first-pass and should be deepened with figure-level extraction of cooling magnitude sensitivities across translation speed and stratification regimes.
