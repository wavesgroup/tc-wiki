# Research Question

Schade and Emanuel (1999) test how ocean feedback, especially storm-induced SST cooling, modifies tropical-cyclone intensity relative to uncoupled atmosphere-only assumptions.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Idealized/simple atmosphere-ocean coupled modeling.
- Comparison design: Coupled vs effectively uncoupled intensity evolution under varying translation speed and upper-ocean thermal structure assumptions.
- Physical pathway emphasized: SST cooling reduces surface enthalpy fluxes, weakening potential for continued deepening.

# Key Equations

Schade and Emanuel (1999) define the ocean-coupling penalty on storm intensity with the SST feedback factor

```math
F_{\mathrm{SST}} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta p|_{\mathrm{SST}}} - 1,
```

where $\Delta p$ is the coupled-model central-pressure deficit and $\Delta p|_{\mathrm{SST}}$ is the pressure deficit from the otherwise identical fixed-SST control run. This is the paper's core reusable diagnostic: more negative $F_{\mathrm{SST}}$ means stronger ocean-induced suppression of intensity.

# Findings

- Schade and Emanuel (1999) found that air-sea coupling can materially reduce simulated tropical-cyclone intensity through storm-induced cold wakes.
- The magnitude of intensity reduction depended on storm speed and ocean mixed-layer characteristics, linking atmospheric intensity evolution to subsurface ocean structure rather than SST alone.
- The paper formalized a practical negative-feedback framing: stronger or slower storms can self-induce cooling that limits further intensification.

# Limitations

- The coupled framework is intentionally simple, so some mesoscale ocean and full-physics atmospheric pathways are parameterized rather than explicitly resolved.
- Basin-dependent validation against large observed storm samples was limited in this first-generation framework.
