# Research Question

Walsh et al. (2002) investigate how hurricane directional wave spectra vary spatially as storms approach or interact with landfall conditions, and how those patterns differ from open-ocean structure.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Hurricane wave-field observational analysis in a landfall context.
- Measurements: Airborne directional wave-spectrum retrieval in storm-relative coordinates.
- Analysis framing: Compare storm-quadrant spectral variability and wave-system partitioning relevant to nearshore hazard conditions.

# Findings

- The paper extends open-ocean directional-spectrum work into the landfall regime, where fetch, storm motion, and coastal proximity complicate wave geometry.
- Subsequent literature cites this study as evidence that wave directionality and spectral shape vary strongly across hurricane quadrants during coastal approach.
- The study is commonly used as an observational benchmark for validating hurricane wave models used in coastal-risk applications.

# Limitations

- This is a first-pass page built from accessible citation-network context; direct full-text extraction is still needed for detailed quantitative findings.
- Landfall-specific sampling may not generalize to all coastlines, bathymetries, or storm translation speeds.
- Coupling implications for drag, spray, and surge require integration with dedicated flux and coastal-inundation studies.
