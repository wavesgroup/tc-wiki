# Citation

Klotzbach, P. J., 2011: *El Niño-Southern Oscillation's impact on Atlantic basin hurricanes and U.S. landfalls*. *Journal of Climate*, 24, 1252-1263. DOI: [10.1175/2010JCLI3799.1](https://doi.org/10.1175/2010JCLI3799.1).

Accessed text source: [Colorado State University-hosted PDF](https://tropical.colostate.edu/Publications/papers/klotzbach_2011_jclim_uslandfalls.pdf).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary research article.

# Research Question

Klotzbach (2011) asks how El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) modulates Atlantic basin hurricane activity and U.S. landfall probability, and whether that modulation differs across Atlantic regions and AMO phases.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Long-record Atlantic hurricane climatology with environmental-composite analysis.
- Storm dataset: Atlantic tropical cyclone statistics from 1900-2009, including named storms, hurricanes, major hurricanes, hurricane days, major-hurricane days, and accumulated cyclone energy (ACE).
- ENSO classification: August-October Niño-3.4 SST anomalies from HadISST, with El Niño and La Niña years defined by +/-0.5 degC thresholds.
- Landfall analysis: U.S. coastal-region and state-impact probabilities for hurricanes and major hurricanes by ENSO phase.
- Environmental diagnostics: NCEP-NCAR reanalysis fields from 1948-2009 over the tropical Atlantic and Caribbean, including 200-850-hPa zonal wind shear, relative humidity, outgoing longwave radiation, sea level pressure, and SST.
- Additional framing: The paper also stratifies results by Atlantic multidecadal oscillation (AMO) phase to assess whether ENSO teleconnections vary across multidecadal Atlantic backgrounds.

# Findings

- Klotzbach (2011) confirms that Atlantic hurricane activity is strongly suppressed in El Niño years and enhanced in La Niña years over the 1900-2009 record.
- The La Niña-to-El Niño ratios grow for stronger metrics: the paper reports ratios of 1.6 for hurricanes, 2.1 for major hurricanes, and 1.9 for ACE.
- The probability of a hyperactive major-hurricane year also shifts sharply with ENSO. The paper reports a 39% chance of four or more Atlantic major hurricanes in La Niña years versus 7% in El Niño years.
- The environmental mechanism is consistent with prior theory but is quantified here over a longer record: El Niño years show stronger upper-level westerlies and greater vertical wind shear, while La Niña years are generally moister and more convectively favorable.
- The Caribbean signal is stronger than over the remainder of the tropical Atlantic. In the paper's reanalysis composites, ENSO-related shear differences are larger there and are accompanied by larger humidity and OLR differences.
- U.S. hurricane landfalls are reduced in El Niño years, with the strongest ENSO contrast along the Florida peninsula and East Coast. North Carolina stands out as one of the states with the largest warm-versus-cold ENSO probability contrast.
- Klotzbach (2011) also argues that ENSO modulation is stronger during the negative AMO phase than during the positive AMO phase.

# Limitations

- The pre-satellite portion of the 1900-2009 hurricane record is less homogeneous than the modern period, especially for weaker storms and some landfall-adjacent basin metrics.
- State-level landfall probabilities are based on relatively rare events, so some subregional probability estimates remain noisy even across a century-scale sample.
- The AMO stratification is descriptive rather than a closed attribution test, so the paper does not isolate why ENSO teleconnections appear stronger in one AMO phase than another.
- The paper is seasonal-statistical rather than storm-structural, so it does not address which storm-scale pathways convert favorable ENSO backgrounds into rapid intensification or major-hurricane survival.
