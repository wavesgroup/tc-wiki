# Citation

Bender, Morris A., and Isaac Ginis, 2000: *Real-Case Simulations of Hurricane-Ocean Interaction Using a High-Resolution Coupled Model: Effects on Hurricane Intensity*. *Monthly Weather Review*, 128(4), 917-946. DOI: [10.1175/1520-0493(2000)128<0917:RCSOHO>2.0.CO;2](https://doi.org/10.1175/1520-0493(2000)128%3C0917:RCSOHO%3E2.0.CO;2).

Accessed text sources: [NOAA/GFDL-hosted PDF](https://www.gfdl.noaa.gov/bibliography/related_files/mb0001.pdf) and publication metadata from [URI repository record](https://digitalcommons.uri.edu/gsofacpubs/1363/).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary coupled forecast-modeling study.

# Research Question

Bender and Ginis (2000) ask whether explicit atmosphere-ocean coupling and realistic cold-wake initialization improve real-case hurricane intensity forecasts.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Real-case coupled model simulations using a high-resolution ocean model linked to a dynamical hurricane forecast model.
- Cases include storms traversing pre-existing or storm-generated cold wakes.
- Evaluation focus: Central pressure/intensity forecast error changes in coupled versus uncoupled configurations.

# Findings

- Bender and Ginis (2000) found that including cold-wake effects improved forecast realism in cases where uncoupled runs over-deepened storms crossing cooled water.
- A key result was that accurate initial SST and upper-ocean structure (including prior-storm wakes) materially changed predicted intensity tendency.
- The study provided early operational evidence that ocean coupling reduces systematic intensity error, especially for storms in moderate intensity ranges where cold-wake feedback is strong.

# Limitations

- Results are model-configuration dependent and reflect the coupled system used at that time.
- Although broader sample statistics were reported, later-generation coupled systems and data assimilation frameworks can alter effect sizes.
