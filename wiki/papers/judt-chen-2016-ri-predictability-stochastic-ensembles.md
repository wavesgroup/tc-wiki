# Citation

Judt, F., and S. S. Chen, 2016: *Predictability and dynamics of tropical cyclone rapid intensification deduced from high-resolution stochastic ensembles*. *Monthly Weather Review*, 144(11), 4395-4420. DOI: [10.1175/MWR-D-15-0413.1](https://doi.org/10.1175/MWR-D-15-0413.1).

Accessed text source: [NSF NCAR/UCAR publication record + abstract](https://impacts.ucar.edu/en/publications/predictability-and-dynamics-of-tropical-cyclone-rapid-intensifica).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary research article.

# Research Question

Judt and Chen (2016) ask how predictable tropical cyclone rapid intensification (RI) is, what controls uncertainty in RI timing, and whether RI follows distinct dynamical pathways even when many forecasts eventually reach major-hurricane intensity.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: High-resolution stochastic-ensemble process and predictability study.
- Storm: Hurricane Earl (2010).
- Ensemble design: Five high-resolution ensembles with scale-dependent stochastic perturbations spanning synoptic to convective scales.
- Framing: Environmental versus internal control of RI, including the role of SST, vertical wind shear, mean-vortex structure, and convective processes.
- Diagnostics: RI timing, eyewall and radius-of-maximum-wind evolution, upper-level warm-core development, and ensemble pathway differences to maturity.

# Findings

- Most ensemble members underwent RI and intensified into major hurricanes, indicating that RI likelihood during Earl's lifetime was comparatively predictable.
- The exact timing of RI was much less predictable and retained a stochastic component, even when eventual major intensification was common across members.
- Judt and Chen (2016) argue that environmental conditions, including SST, help control maximum intensity and RI likelihood over the storm lifetime, while both environmental and internal processes contribute to RI-timing uncertainty.
- Two dominant RI modes emerged: an early-RI pathway in which rapid contraction of the radius of maximum wind accompanied eyewall development, and a late-RI pathway in which a well-developed eyewall already existed before RI while an upper-level warm core formed during the RI episode.
- Inference for this wiki: RI forecast difficulty is partly a pathway problem rather than only a threshold problem, because storms can be predisposed toward RI while still having low predictability in its onset time and structural route.

# Limitations

- This is a single-storm ensemble study, so its early-versus-late RI pathways are not yet a basinwide taxonomy by themselves.
- The work depends on the chosen perturbation framework and model configuration.
- The accessible source here is publication-record abstract constrained; full-text ingestion would improve the dynamical detail for warm-core, eyewall, and radius-contraction diagnostics.
- The paper diagnoses RI timing uncertainty but does not directly validate those modes against a large observational sample.
