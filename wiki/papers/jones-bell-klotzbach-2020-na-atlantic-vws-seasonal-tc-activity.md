# Citation

Jones, J. J., M. M. Bell, and P. J. Klotzbach, 2020: *Tropical and subtropical North Atlantic vertical wind shear and seasonal tropical cyclone activity*. *Journal of Climate*, 33, 5413-5426. DOI: [10.1175/JCLI-D-19-0474.1](https://doi.org/10.1175/JCLI-D-19-0474.1).

Accessed text source: [Colorado State University publication summary and abstract](https://tropical.colostate.edu/pub/jones2020tropical.html).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary research article.

# Research Question

Jones et al. (2020) ask how much of seasonal tropical North Atlantic vertical wind shear variability is driven by tropical climate modes versus subtropical anticyclonic Rossby wave breaking (AWB), and how those shear modes relate to seasonal tropical cyclone activity.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Seasonal climate-diagnostic study of Atlantic environmental controls on tropical cyclone activity.
- Core variable: Boreal-summer tropical North Atlantic zonal vertical wind shear, treated as a key large-scale seasonal predictor.
- Main method: Empirical orthogonal function (EOF) analysis of July-September tropical North Atlantic zonal vertical wind shear anomalies.
- Attribution framing: The leading principal components are compared with climate indices associated with ENSO, the Atlantic meridional mode (AMM), anticyclonic Rossby wave breaking, Walker circulation, and Sahel rainfall.
- Activity linkage: Principal components are regressed against tropical North Atlantic vertical wind shear structure and accumulated cyclone energy (ACE).
- Additional diagnostic: An AWB-associated vertical-wind-shear anomaly index is tested as a proxy for AWB influence on the large-scale Atlantic environment.

# Findings

- Jones et al. (2020) find that ENSO primarily drives the leading mode of tropical North Atlantic zonal vertical wind shear variability.
- The paper identifies subtropical anticyclonic Rossby wave breaking as the driver of the second mode of variability, accounting for about 23% of the structured variance in the analyzed shear field.
- The authors argue that extratropical AWB is not a minor side influence: it materially contributes to both tropical and subtropical Atlantic shear variability and therefore to seasonal TC activity.
- Linear-regression tests show that the leading mode carries most of the ACE regression strength, but adding the second and third modes improves the seasonal relationship.
- The AWB-associated shear-anomaly index performs as a useful large-scale proxy for summertime shear anomalies and helps isolate a pathway by which extratropical circulation can modulate Atlantic seasonal activity.
- Inference for this wiki: this paper gives Klotzbach's broader Atlantic seasonal-forecast branch a more explicit mechanism, showing that seasonal predictability is not only a tropical ENSO problem but also a tropical-extratropical shear-pattern problem.

# Limitations

- This is a basin-specific seasonal-climate study, so its results do not directly translate to storm-scale rapid intensification or case-specific track/intensity forecasts.
- EOF attribution is a pattern-analysis tool rather than a closed dynamical budget, so the identified modes should be treated as physically suggestive structures, not unique causes.
- The AWB metric is an environmental proxy and therefore does not resolve exactly how individual breaking events couple into genesis, intensification, or landfall pathways.
- Current ingest is abstract-constrained rather than full-text extracted, so fine-grained methodological details should be revisited if this branch becomes central to later forecast-skill synthesis.
