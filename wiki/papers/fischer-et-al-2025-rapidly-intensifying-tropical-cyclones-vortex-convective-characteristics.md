# Citation

Fischer, M. S., P. D. Reasor, J. P. Dunion, and R. F. Rogers, 2025: *Are rapidly intensifying tropical cyclones associated with unique vortex and convective characteristics?* *Monthly Weather Review*, 153, 183-203. DOI: [10.1175/MWR-D-24-0118.1](https://doi.org/10.1175/MWR-D-24-0118.1).

Accessed text source: [NOAA Institutional Repository PDF](https://repository.library.noaa.gov/view/noaa/67481/noaa_67481_DS1.pdf).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary research article.

# Research Question

Fischer et al. (2025) ask whether tropical cyclones that undergo rapid intensification have distinctive vortex and convective structure relative to storms of similar current intensity that either intensify more slowly or fail to intensify.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Multicase observational composite analysis using airborne Doppler radar analyses plus environmental metadata.
- Basin and sampling scope: Overwater tropical cyclones sampled by NOAA WP-3D aircraft over roughly the last three decades, primarily in the North Atlantic.
- Core dataset: Version `v3k` of the TC-RADAR database, using merged analyses for azimuthal-mean vortex structure and swath analyses for convective-burst and vertical-mass-flux distributions.
- Case partitioning: Future 12-h intensity change groups defined from best track as rapid intensification (RI; >= 20 kt), slowly intensifying (SI; > 0 and < 20 kt), and nonintensifying (NI; <= 0 kt).
- Sample size: 132 merged analyses total, comprising 22 RI, 75 SI, and 35 NI cases.
- Screening choices: Cases within 50 km of land during the 12-h period, cases stronger than 95 kt at onset, and cases within 40 kt of their environmental maximum potential intensity are excluded so the sample retains thermodynamic headroom for RI.
- Structural normalization: The paper diagnoses anomalous structure relative to the typical structure for storms of similar intensity, using a hybrid radius referenced to the radius of maximum wind at 2 km height.
- Diagnostics analyzed: Azimuthally averaged tangential wind, relative vorticity, radial wind, vertical mass flux, vortex tilt magnitude, convective-burst counts and eyewall coverage, a combined vortex-favorability metric, and an environmental ventilation proxy derived from SHIPS variables.

# Findings

- Fischer et al. (2025) find that RI episodes are associated with primary circulations that are anomalously taller and narrower than both SI and NI episodes when compared at similar storm intensity.
- The paper finds that RI cases also have significantly smaller vortex tilt magnitudes than SI and NI cases, consistent with more vertically aligned vortices.
- RI episodes exhibit an anomalously deep overturning circulation: weaker outflow between about 5 and 10 km outward of the radius of maximum wind, stronger upper-tropospheric outflow above about 10 km, and larger azimuthally averaged ascent in the mid-upper troposphere.
- A key difference between RI and SI cases is the depth of the anomalous ascent layer. SI storms show some upper-level ascent enhancement, but RI storms sustain it through a deeper layer of the inner core.
- NI cases display the shallow opposite structure: stronger lower- and midtropospheric outflow just above the inflow layer, relatively weak upper-level ascent, and shallow broad vortices. The authors therefore argue that vigorous low-level ascent by itself is not sufficient for intensification.
- RI storms have substantially more convective-burst activity in the eyewall region. The median number of convective bursts in RI cases is more than double that of SI and NI cases, and the fractional eyewall coverage by convective bursts is also significantly larger.
- The paper finds that RI occurs preferentially when both the internal vortex structure is favorable and the synoptic-scale environment is favorable. For a given environmental favorability, larger vortex-favorability values are associated with greater future intensification rates.
- The authors hypothesize that relatively narrow, compact RI vortices with weaker outer-core inertial stability help promote strong inner-core inflow and ascent, but they present this as a physically motivated interpretation rather than a directly closed causal proof.

# Limitations

- The sample is primarily North Atlantic and based on storms observed by NOAA aircraft, so basin generality is not yet established.
- The study excludes near-land cases, storms stronger than 95 kt at the start of the period, and storms close to their MPI ceiling, so the results do not span the full tropical cyclone intensity spectrum.
- Airborne Doppler radar analyses do not fully resolve the lowest 500 m of the boundary layer, where peak inflow usually occurs, and the outflow layer is only partially observed where radar scatterers are sparse.
- The study analyzes 12-h intensity-change episodes rather than a full RI-onset-relative chronology, so it cannot determine cleanly whether the anomalous vortex structure precedes RI onset or is partly produced by the RI process.
- The structural metrics are intensity-relative composites and selected diagnostic domains rather than universal dynamical laws, so threshold choices and sampling geometry remain part of the uncertainty.
- Inference for this wiki: the paper provides strong multicase observational support for tall, narrow, aligned, deeply overturning RI-favorable vortices, but it does not by itself resolve the causal sequence linking vortex alignment, boundary-layer inflow, convective bursts, and RI onset.
