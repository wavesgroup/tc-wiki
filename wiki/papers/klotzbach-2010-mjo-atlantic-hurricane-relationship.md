# Citation

Klotzbach, P. J., 2010: *On the Madden-Julian Oscillation-Atlantic hurricane relationship*. *Journal of Climate*, 23, 282-293. DOI: [10.1175/2009JCLI2978.1](https://doi.org/10.1175/2009JCLI2978.1).

Accessed text source: [Colorado State University-hosted PDF](https://tropical.colostate.edu/Publications/papers/klotzbach_2010.pdf).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary research article.

# Research Question

Klotzbach (2010) asks how Madden-Julian oscillation (MJO) phase modulates Atlantic tropical cyclone genesis, intensification, and U.S. landfall likelihood on intraseasonal time scales.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Atlantic intraseasonal climatology linking MJO phase to storm activity and large-scale environmental anomalies.
- MJO definition: Wheeler-Hendon real-time multivariate MJO index, using 1974-2007 data and excluding 1978 when outgoing-longwave-radiation data were unavailable.
- Storm dataset: Atlantic HURDAT tropical cyclones, with subtropical cyclones excluded.
- Environmental fields: NCEP-NCAR reanalysis zonal winds, vertical wind shear, sea level pressure, relative humidity, and sea surface temperature proxies.
- Regional partitions: Basinwide Atlantic activity, main development region activity, and a northwest Caribbean/Gulf of Mexico genesis subset.
- Comparison framing: Counts were normalized by days spent in each MJO phase, with special attention to phases 1-2 versus phases 6-7 and to periods when MJO amplitude exceeded one standard deviation.

# Findings

- Klotzbach (2010) finds that Atlantic tropical cyclone activity is strongly modulated by MJO phase, with phases 1-2 substantially more favorable than phases 6-7 for genesis and intensification.
- The paper links the favorable phases to reduced vertical wind shear, weaker upper-level westerlies, and moister midlevels across the tropical Atlantic.
- In the main development region, the favorable MJO phases produce many more storms that later become hurricanes and major hurricanes than the unfavorable phases.
- The regional landfall-proximate signal is also large: roughly four to five times as many named storms form in the northwest Caribbean and Gulf of Mexico in phases 1-2 as in phases 6-7.
- Major-hurricane activity is especially sensitive to phase. The paper reports 91.5 major-hurricane days in phases 1-2 versus 20.5 in phases 6-7 over the 1974-2007 sample.
- U.S. landfalls are likewise modulated by MJO phase. The paper argues that genesis-location changes dominate this signal more clearly than any systematic steering-pattern change diagnosed in the analysis.
- Inference for this wiki: Klotzbach (2010) provides an operationally useful bridge between weather-scale forecasting and seasonal outlooks by showing that Atlantic TC activity has a meaningful subseasonal climate control, not only a seasonal one.

# Limitations

- The study is climatological and phase-composite based, so it identifies robust environmental modulation but does not close the causal chain from MJO phase to individual-storm structure or intensity change.
- The record begins in 1974 and excludes 1978, limiting sample size for rare outcomes such as major-hurricane landfalls.
- The analysis uses reanalysis environmental fields and normalized counts, which are useful for large-scale interpretation but do not resolve storm-scale convective pathways.
- MJO predictability in boreal summer is limited relative to winter, so the forecast value implied by the paper is probabilistic and lead-time dependent rather than deterministic.
