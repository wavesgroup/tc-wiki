# Citation

Chen, Shuyi S., and Milan Curcic, 2016: *Ocean surface waves in Hurricane Ike (2008) and Superstorm Sandy (2012): Coupled model predictions and observations*. *Ocean Modelling*, 103, 161-176. DOI: [10.1016/j.ocemod.2015.08.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ocemod.2015.08.005).

Accessed text sources: Author-hosted PDF copy and Crossref DOI metadata.

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary coupled hurricane-wave modeling and observation-evaluation study.

# Research Question

Chen and Curcic (2016) ask how well a fully coupled atmosphere-wave-ocean model can predict hurricane wave structure in open-ocean and near-landfall settings, and which shallow-water/coastal processes most strongly reshape the wave field.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Coupled-model evaluation and process diagnosis.
- Cases: Hurricane Ike (2008) in the Gulf of Mexico and Superstorm Sandy (2012) along the northeastern U.S. coast.
- Model framework: High-resolution UWIN-CM atmosphere-wave-ocean simulations or forecasts run 3-5 days in advance.
- Observations: NDBC buoy winds and waves plus Jason-2 satellite altimeter measurements.
- Diagnostics: Significant wave height, mean wave period, directional spectra, wind-wave misalignment, and inferred drag behavior near landfall.

# Findings

- Chen and Curcic (2016) found that the coupled model reproduced observed winds and waves well in both storms, with especially strong performance in Sandy's unusual large-wave pattern.
- As Ike and Sandy approached landfall, the directional spectra broadened and became more complex than in the open ocean because shoaling, dissipation, refraction, and opposing swell all became important in shallow water.
- The paper shows that wind-wave misalignment increased strongly near landfall, reaching about 30 degrees in Ike and about 60 degrees in Sandy in the most affected quadrants, and that the combined shallow-water and opposing-swell effects increased modeled drag by up to about 25% in Ike.
- The front-left quadrant emerged as the most complex sector near landfall, where offshore wind, incoming swell, and coastal topography made simple wave parameterization least reliable.

# Limitations

- The paper examines only two storms, and both the spectral complexity and stress response remain sensitive to storm-specific bathymetry, coastal geometry, and wind-field asymmetry.
- The drag implications are inferred from a coupled model and supporting observations rather than from direct momentum-flux closure measurements made in the landfall zone itself.
- Forecast track and intensity errors still propagate into the modeled wave field, especially near landfall where gradients are sharp.
