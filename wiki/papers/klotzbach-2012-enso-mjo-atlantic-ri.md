# Citation

Klotzbach, P. J., 2012: *El Niño-Southern Oscillation, the Madden-Julian Oscillation and Atlantic basin tropical cyclone rapid intensification*. *Journal of Geophysical Research: Atmospheres*, 117, D14104. DOI: [10.1029/2012JD017714](https://doi.org/10.1029/2012JD017714).

Accessed text source: [Colorado State University-hosted PDF](https://tropical.colostate.edu/Publications/papers/klotzbach_2012.pdf).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary research article.

# Research Question

Klotzbach (2012) asks whether the known ENSO and MJO controls on Atlantic basin tropical cyclone activity also extend specifically to rapid intensification (RI) episodes.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Atlantic rapid-intensification climatology tied to interannual and intraseasonal climate modes.
- Study period: 1974-2010, excluding 1978 because the Wheeler-Hendon MJO index is unavailable for part of that year.
- ENSO definition: Multivariate ENSO Index (MEI), with the ten strongest warm years classified as El Niño and the ten strongest cold years classified as La Niña.
- MJO definition: Wheeler-Hendon MJO index, with phase-based comparisons emphasized for days when the index amplitude exceeds one standard deviation.
- Storm dataset: Atlantic HURDAT best track.
- RI metrics: 24-h intensification thresholds of 25, 30, 35, and 40 kt, with events counted only when the storm was at least 35 kt at the start of the 24-h window.
- Environmental diagnostics: NCEP-NCAR reanalysis fields and reanalysis skin temperature used as a sea-surface-temperature proxy across the Atlantic main development region.

# Findings

- Klotzbach (2012) finds that the ENSO and MJO signals previously documented for basinwide activity also extend clearly to Atlantic rapid intensification.
- The paper reports nearly three times as many Atlantic RI events in La Niña years as in El Niño years.
- MJO phase exerts an even stronger intraseasonal modulation in the preferred comparison. Approximately four times more RI episodes occur when strong-amplitude MJO convection is in phases 1-2 than when it is in phases 6-7.
- Storms forming in MJO phases 1-2 are about twice as likely to undergo at least one RI episode during their lifetime as storms forming in phases 6-7.
- The combined ENSO-MJO framing is more discriminating than either mode alone. The paper argues that the most favorable RI environments occur when favorable ENSO and favorable MJO phases align.
- The paper links these RI contrasts to coherent large-scale differences in vertical wind shear, midlevel moisture, sea level pressure, and temperature over the main development region.
- Inference for this wiki: Klotzbach (2012) adds a climate-mode branch to the RI problem, implying that even if RI remains storm-structural and partially stochastic, its basinwide probability is still materially conditioned by large-scale intraseasonal and interannual background state.

# Limitations

- The study is basinwide and climatological, so it does not resolve whether climate modes act mainly through genesis location, inner-core structure, ventilation history, or RI persistence once RI has begun.
- The RI sample is moderate in size and limited to the 1974-2010 satellite-era period, which constrains more detailed phase and threshold stratifications.
- Reanalysis skin temperature is used as an SST proxy, which is acceptable for large-scale composite work but is not equivalent to storm-relative upper-ocean diagnostics.
- The paper uses a phase/environment framework rather than direct storm-core observations, so it should be treated as a background-state constraint on RI probability rather than a complete RI mechanism paper.
