# Research Question

Nolan et al. (2007) ask how much tropical-cyclone intensification can be produced by purely asymmetric convective heating versus the azimuthally averaged component of that heating, and how efficiently injected heat is converted into kinetic-energy increase of the symmetric vortex.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Idealized theoretical-numerical modeling analysis of balanced-vortex response to localized diabatic heating.
- Model framing: Updated linear/weakly nonlinear framework for asymmetric heating response with equations reformulated to be consistent with anelastic dynamics and a radially varying reference state.
- Forcing design: Tests include instantaneous and time-evolving asymmetric heat sources, including heat sources that move with the local mean wind.
- Diagnostics: Symmetric and asymmetric kinetic/available-potential-energy budgets, transient growth diagnostics, and axisymmetrization effects of heating-generated potential-vorticity anomalies.
- Efficiency metric: Heat-to-kinetic conversion efficiency following Hack and Schubert-style formulations, compared across vortex intensity/size regimes and Coriolis parameter.

# Findings

- Nolan et al. (2007) reaffirm that intensity change from localized heating is dominated by the azimuthal-mean heating component; the purely asymmetric component typically contributes little and often weakens the symmetric vortex.
- In experiments with evolving asymmetric heat sources, vortex weakening is linked to transient growth of asymmetric disturbances that extract energy from the symmetric vortex through downgradient momentum transport across radial and vertical wind shears.
- Axisymmetrization returns part of that energy to the symmetric circulation, but the net energy change is commonly negative in their baseline configurations.
- Some parameter variations (including altered heating duration/rotation behavior and more barotropic background-vortex structure) can produce asymmetric-forced intensification, but those effects remain small relative to the symmetric-response contribution from azimuthally averaged heating.
- The diagnosed intensification efficiency increases with vortex intensity and with Coriolis parameter; the authors also report substantial efficiency increases for weak vortices.

# Limitations

- This is an idealized modeling and energetics study, so direct transfer to fully nonlinear, fully moist, observed tropical cyclones is not automatic.
- The accessible ingestion source is abstract-level rather than full article text, so this page should be treated as a constrained first-pass synthesis pending full-text extraction.
- Reported conclusions emphasize small-perturbation and controlled-forcing limits; real convective organization, air-sea coupling, and environmental flow may alter relative symmetric versus asymmetric contributions.
