Accessed text sources: [NOAA/AOML Hurricane Research Division publications page](https://www.aoml.noaa.gov/hrd/about_hrd/publications.html) and the [Amanote article record](https://research.amanote.com/publication/II8n1HMBKQvf0BhiVgDa/turbulent-fluxes-in-the-hurricane-boundary-layer-part-ii-latent-heat-flux).

Peer-review status: Peer-reviewed primary research article.

# Research Question

Drennan et al. (2007) ask how latent heat flux and the Dalton number behave in the hurricane boundary layer when measured directly from aircraft turbulence observations.

# Data And Methods

- Study type: Direct observational turbulence analysis.
- Storm sample: Hurricanes Fabian (2003) and Isabel (2003).
- Platform: Instrumented aircraft flights during the CBLAST-hurricane field season.
- Primary diagnostics: Latent heat flux, humidity flux, and Dalton number in the high-wind hurricane boundary layer.
- Wind regime: Measurements extend to 10-m-equivalent winds up to about 30 m s^-1.

# Findings

- Drennan et al. (2007) report the first direct measurements of latent heat flux in the hurricane boundary layer.
- The paper states that the accessible wind-speed range for humidity fluxes and Dalton numbers was extended by more than 50% relative to earlier observations.
- Up to 30 m s^-1, the highest 10-m winds measured, the Dalton number is reported to be not significantly different from the HEXOS result.
- The authors report no evidence that the Dalton number increases with wind speed over the observed range.

# Limitations

- The study is limited to two storms and does not directly sample the most extreme major-hurricane wind regime.
- The conclusions concern latent heat exchange only; momentum exchange and combined enthalpy implications require the companion and follow-on papers.
- Because the analysis is based on direct flux runs in severe conditions, sample size remains limited and may not span the full range of wave-state or precipitation environments.
